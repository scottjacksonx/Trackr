The Regular-Length Version

Hi there. I'm Scott. This year, I'm doing a research project at UQ that's centred around the phenomenon of Liking things online. Almost every site that's even vaguely social has something resembling a Like button — Facebook has Likes, Twitter has Favorites, Reddit has upvotes, et cetera. Millions of people use these buttons millions of times every day. I want to look at how and why people use Like buttons, and what kind of things we can do with that information to enhance recommendation systems. Here's the short version:

In the physical world, one of the ways we define ourselves is through our taste — we wear Nike shoes instead of Adidas, use Apple computers instead of Dells, or wear t-shirts with nerd slogans on them. We present ourselves to the world by associating ourselves with what we like. What's the online equivalent to this? How do we define our online identity? I posit that we do it with Like buttons.

Once I've got some information about why people use Like buttons, I want to use that data to try and enhance recommendation engines that work by using your Likes as a seed. Currently, these recommendation engines work by looking at what you Like and recommending content to you. I want to make a recommendation engine that bases its recommendations not only on what you Like, but also why you Like.

OK. So, why am I here?

I need some help. Part of my research project is a study that looks at why people Like things online. I need to gather some statistics on why people are Liking things. To that end, I've put together some browser extensions. Basically, install one of these browser extensions and whenever you Like something on sites like Reddit, Tumblr, Flickr, YouTube, or Twitter, the browser extension will pop up a window and ask you a couple of questions about why you Liked what you just Liked. When you've answered the questions, those answers get put into my database.

Is this safe? How do I know you're not going to be a dick with my data?

The Like data I collect is all anonymous. Nothing links you personally to the things that you've Liked. At the end of the project, I will delete all of the data that I've gathered. Quite simply, there's no way for me to be nefarious with this data. However, if you still have concerns, you can inspect the code for yourself — the whole project (sans the recorded data, of course) is up on GitHub. If you're really, really interested in my project, feel free to read my proposal.
